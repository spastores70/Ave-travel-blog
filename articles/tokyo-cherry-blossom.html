Tokyo in Cherry Blossom Season: A Complete Guide

February 8, 2025 | 8 min read | Tokyo, Japan

I have been to Tokyo three times. The first in autumn, when the gingkos turned gold. The second in winter, when the city felt like a perfectly organized dream. But nothing — nothing — prepared me for Tokyo in late March, when the sakura bloom and the entire city turns pink.

Cherry blossom season, hanami, is one of those things that sounds overhyped until you're standing beneath a canopy of blossoms at Shinjuku Gyoen with a can of cold coffee, watching salarymen spread out blue picnic tarps beside grandmothers in kimonos and university students in matching pink headbands. Then you understand. Japan takes beauty seriously in a way that feels almost radical.

Timing Your Visit

The bloom is famously unpredictable. The Japan Meteorological Corporation releases annual sakura forecasts, and the national obsession with these predictions is both charming and useful. In Tokyo, peak bloom (mankai) typically falls in the last week of March or the first week of April, but climate shifts have pushed it earlier in recent years. I arrived March 25th and had five perfect days — anything earlier would have been too early; a week later and the petals were beginning to fall.

Book accommodation at least four months in advance. I cannot stress this enough. Hotel prices triple and availability collapses. I stayed in Yanaka, the old shitamachi quarter — a quieter, more residential choice that meant the trains were less crushed and I woke to temple bells rather than hotel corridors.

The Meguro River lined with cherry trees — one of Tokyo's most iconic hanami spots.

The Best Hanami Spots

Every neighborhood has its own parks and canal walks draped in blossom. These were my favourites, ranked by how un-crowded they felt at different times of day:

  • Shinjuku Gyoen — The grandest collection of trees in the city (over 1,000). Arrive at 9am when it opens and you will have a quiet hour before the crowds. No alcohol allowed, which keeps the atmosphere calm and genuinely beautiful.
  • Chidorigafuchi — A moat around the Imperial Palace where rowboats drift beneath overhanging branches. The queue for boats runs long; the walk along the bank is free and equally lovely at dusk.
  • Meguro River — The canal is flanked by coffee shops and wine bars. Hanami at night here, with lanterns illuminating the blossoms, is one of the most romantic things I have ever witnessed.
  • Yanaka Cemetery — Locals only. A moss-covered graveyard where enormous old trees shed petals onto stone lanterns. Quiet, melancholic, and extraordinary.

"Japan takes beauty seriously in a way that feels almost radical. The cherry blossom season is proof of that — a whole nation stopping to notice something fleeting."

Beyond the Blossoms

Cherry blossom season is a perfect excuse to do all the Tokyo things you might otherwise rush. Slow down at Yanaka's pottery studios and handmade chopstick shops. Take the Yamanote Line one full loop at dusk when every station briefly frames a different version of the city. Visit the Mori Art Museum in Roppongi Hills — the views of the city from the observation deck, with the pink tree canopy visible far below, are worth the ticket price alone.

I also made time for the Hama-rikyu Gardens, a tidal garden near Tsukiji where plum trees bloom early and the contrast between traditional garden architecture and the skyscraper skyline is startling. The tea house in the center serves matcha and wagashi sweets for a few hundred yen. I sat for an hour and watched herons and suited businessmen in roughly equal measure.

A neighborhood shrine in Yanaka — the blossom petals collect in every corner like pink snow.

What to Eat During Hanami

Convenience store onigiri eaten under a cherry tree on a cold morning is a genuinely perfect meal. Don't let anyone convince you otherwise. Lawson and 7-Eleven both release sakura-themed seasonal items: cherry blossom mochi, pink milk bread, strawberry sandos. Embrace all of it.

For sit-down meals: Yanaka Ginza has small restaurants and yakitori stalls that spill onto the street. In Shinjuku, the Memory Lane (Omoide Yokocho) alley of tiny grilled-skewer bars fills with smoke and laughter every evening from 6pm. Reservations at most are impossible; just join a queue and wait. The best things in Tokyo require patience, and they are always worth it.

Practical Notes

Get a Suica card from any JR machine on arrival — it works on all trains, buses, and even convenience store purchases. Download the Japan Official Travel App for real-time sakura forecasts. Pack layers: Tokyo in late March can swing between 8°C and 20°C within the same day.
