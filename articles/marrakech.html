Lost in the Medina: 48 Hours in Marrakech

March 15, 2025 | 7 min read | Marrakech, Morocco

The taxi driver dropped me outside the Djemaa el-Fna at dusk and said, simply, "Bonne chance." Good luck. I didn't understand why until I took my first step into the medina and the walls swallowed me whole.

Marrakech is a city that operates on its own logic. Streets that appear on maps do not exist; streets that exist do not appear on maps. Alleys fold into courtyards that fold into other alleys. Within ten minutes of leaving my riad, I was completely, gloriously lost — and I have been chasing that feeling in cities ever since.

Getting Lost is the Point

Every travel guide will tell you to navigate by landmark: the Koutoubia Mosque, the spice market, the dyers' quarter. Good advice. My advice is to ignore it for at least one afternoon. Put your phone away, pick a direction that smells interesting, and walk. You will stumble into a baker pulling flatbreads from a clay oven, or a carpenter shaping cedar into lattice screens, or a quiet fountain courtyard where cats sleep in the afternoon sun. These are the moments Marrakech gives you when you stop trying to find anything specific.

The medina is divided into quarters roughly by trade. Leatherworkers cluster near the tanneries; coppersmiths ring their hammers in the metal souk; fabric sellers drape silks across doorways like theatre curtains. Even if you cannot afford to buy, the visual rhythm of each quarter is extraordinary.

The spice souk near Rahba Kedima — an overload of color and scent in the best possible way.

The Souks at Sensory Overload

There is a moment in the central souks — somewhere between the lantern sellers and the carpet merchants — where your senses simply short-circuit. Cumin and cedar and tanned leather and incense hit you all at once. Someone is playing a small drum. A moped squeezes past with impossible confidence. A boy with a tray of mint tea appears from nowhere. You are awake in a way that no amount of coffee ever achieves.

The carpet sellers will invite you in for tea. Accept. The tea is genuinely good and the ritual is genuine hospitality, not just a sales tactic — though yes, they will show you rugs. You are under no obligation to buy. I sat for forty minutes in a shop near Mouassine, drank three glasses of mint tea, learned the difference between Berber, Kilim, and Beni Ourain weaves, and left with only a handshake. The owner seemed entirely unbothered.

"Cumin and cedar and tanned leather and incense hit you all at once. You are awake in a way that no amount of coffee ever achieves."

Jemaa el-Fna After Dark

During the day, the main square is a dusty expanse of snake charmers, henna artists, and orange juice vendors. Charming enough. But return at nightfall and the square transforms entirely. Food stalls materialize from nowhere, each one competing with its neighbor through shouted invitations, theatrical pricing boards, and the smell of lamb merguez and harira soup. The whole thing is illuminated by bare bulbs and the glow of phone screens. It is loud and pushy and magnificent.

I ate at stall number 32 on the recommendation of my riad owner — lamb tagine with preserved lemon and a basket of warm bread for under 60 dirhams. Across from me, a Moroccan family was celebrating something; the grandmother kept refilling everyone's bowls. A storyteller worked the crowd in Darija near the center, and even without understanding a word, I stayed for twenty minutes just to watch the audience's faces.

The inner courtyard of my riad — a cool, tiled sanctuary from the medina's heat and noise.

Day Trip: The Agafay Desert

Forty minutes outside Marrakech, the city releases you into something unexpected: a rocky moonscape of grey and ochre hills, far quieter than the Sahara but no less striking. The Agafay is not a sand desert — it is a stone desert, studded with argan trees and the occasional shepherd's path. I rented a camel for an hour and felt entirely ridiculous and entirely happy about it.

If you have one full day to spare, book a sunrise camel ride, watch the Atlas Mountains glow pink above the horizon, and take coffee at one of the desert camp restaurants before the day heats up. Back in Marrakech by midday, you will feel as though you have been away for a week.

Staying and Eating Well

A riad — a traditional house built around a central courtyard — is the only way to stay. Mine was near Bab Doukkala: eight rooms, a rooftop terrace, breakfast that appeared like magic each morning with msemen flatbreads and argan oil and a bowl of amlou paste. Budget for a mid-range riad. The difference between the cheapest and a modest splurge is meaningful: soundproofing, hot water pressure, a functioning fan in summer.

For food beyond Jemaa el-Fna: Café des Épices for lunch overlooking the Rahba Kedima square; Nomad for an elevated take on Moroccan classics; and the street vendors near the Mouassine fountain for a merguez sandwich that costs almost nothing and tastes like a revelation.

Practical Notes

Best months: October to April. Summer is genuinely brutal (40°C+). Carry small change — 10 and 20 dirham notes — for street food and tips. Most riads will arrange airport transfers; use them for the first arrival to avoid aggressive taxi negotiation while jet-lagged.
