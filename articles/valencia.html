A Day of Orange Light in Valencia

January 18, 2026 | 5 min read | Valencia, Spain

Valencia is a city that feels slightly overlooked, and I mean that as the highest possible compliment. While Madrid is grand and Barcelona is spectacular and Seville is soulful, Valencia just gets on with it — cooking extraordinary food, building improbably beautiful architecture, and filling its plazas with unhurried afternoon life. I went in January, when the orange trees along every street were heavy with fruit and the light had that particular Spanish winter quality: horizontal and amber and lasting all afternoon.

I had one full day, planned nothing specific, and was constantly surprised. This is the kind of city that rewards the aimless.

The Old City at First Light

The Barrio del Carmen — the medieval heart of Valencia, built inside the old city walls — is best at 8am, before the cafés fully open and before the tourists have found it. The streets are narrow and winding with stone archways and small squares where old men sit with coffee and the fountain goes and a cat or two investigates the night's leftovers. The light at that hour comes down between the buildings in long, specific shafts. I walked for ninety minutes and did not want to stop.

Breakfast: a café con leche and a fresh-squeezed orange juice from a bar near the Torres de Serranos — the medieval gate at the city's north entrance. The orange juice in Valencia is different from everywhere else. It comes from the same Valencian oranges that line the streets, and it tastes bright and slightly bitter in the best possible way.

The Barrio del Carmen at morning — stone walls, orange trees, and almost no one else around.

La Lonja de la Seda

La Lonja de la Seda (the Silk Exchange) is a UNESCO World Heritage building from the fifteenth century and one of the most beautiful interiors in Spain. The trading hall is a single enormous room supported by spiral columns that twist upward like stone braids, with a ceiling of intricate Gothic vaulting. It was built to house the booming silk trade and was, at the time of its construction, the largest secular building in Europe.

Entry costs €2. I walked in and stood still for a long time. This is what European cities have that nowhere else quite manages: buildings of extraordinary ambition and craft, sitting quietly in the middle of an ordinary neighbourhood, costing almost nothing to enter.

"Valencia just gets on with it — cooking extraordinary food, building improbably beautiful architecture, and filling its plazas with unhurried afternoon life."

The Real Paella

Paella is Valencian, not Spanish in some generic sense — it was invented in the rice paddies south of the city, and the Valencians are quietly territorial about this. Real Valencian paella contains chicken and rabbit (sometimes snails), green beans, and flat white beans. It does not contain seafood. Seafood paella is a separate and legitimate dish, but it is not the original.

The restaurants near the beach (La Malvarrosa) serve the best versions at lunch. I ate at a restaurant recommended by my Airbnb host — a seafront place with plastic chairs and paper tablecloths where the paella is made for a minimum of two people and takes forty-five minutes. The rice at the bottom is slightly charred (socorrat) and slightly crisp, which is intentional and correct. I ate the entire pan.

Traditional Valencian paella — wood fire, flat pan, a 40-minute wait, and absolutely worth every minute.

El Carmen at Night

After a two-hour post-paella walk along the beach (necessary), I returned to El Carmen for the evening. The neighbourhood transforms at night: the craft shops close and the bars open and the small squares fill with people of all ages drinking vermut and cañas and talking with the particular unhurried sociability of Valencian evenings.

I found a bar with a ceramic-tiled counter and forty types of vermouth on the shelf and drank one cold with an olive and a slice of orange. Outside, the night was mild for January and the streets were lit by the glow from café windows. Somewhere someone was playing guitar. I sat in a square for a long time and felt very much in the right place.

Practical Notes

Valencia is an easy day trip from Madrid (1hr 40min on the AVE high-speed train) or an overnight from Barcelona. The best months are March–June and September–November. In January you will have the city almost to yourself. Book paella restaurants at the beach in advance even in low season — they fill with locals at Sunday lunch regardless of time of year.
