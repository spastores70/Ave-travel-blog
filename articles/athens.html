Rooftops and Ruins in Athens

November 9, 2025 | 6 min read | Athens, Greece

Athens is the city that shouldn't work and does. It is chaotic and slightly crumbling in places; the traffic is genuinely alarming; the graffiti covers entire city blocks. And then you turn a corner and the Acropolis is just there, above everything, ancient and impossible and gold in the afternoon light, and you understand that Athens has been confounding people like this for two and a half thousand years and has no intention of stopping.

I went in November and found it close to perfect. The tourist crowds had thinned, the temperatures were mild (18–22°C), and the ancient sites were quiet enough to actually think in. I stayed for five nights and left wanting more.

The Acropolis at Opening Time

The Acropolis opens at 8am. Be there at 8am. Not 9am, not 9:30 — 8am, when the morning light is still slanted and the stone of the Parthenon glows amber and the tour groups have not yet arrived. For the first hour you will share the site with perhaps fifty other people. For the second hour, the crowds begin. By 10am it is transformed.

The Parthenon is, of course, the point — a building that has been partially destroyed, partially dismantled, used as a mosque, used as an ammunition dump, and still radiates a structural intelligence and beauty that stops you cold. But I spent nearly as long at the Erechtheion, the temple with the porch supported by female figures (the Caryatids), and at the belvedere on the south slope where you can look down over the whole city and the sea beyond and understand, viscerally, why people chose to build a city here.

The Acropolis at 8am — the stones still warm from the previous day, the city below just beginning to wake.

Monastiraki and the Flea Market

The Monastiraki neighbourhood at the foot of the Acropolis is part flea market, part tourist bazaar, part genuinely good neighbourhood with excellent coffee shops and tavernas. On Sunday mornings the flea market spreads across multiple streets and squares — old coins, Byzantine icons, mid-century furniture, military surplus, vinyl records, and handmade jewellery, all mixed together with the energy of a city that does not take commerce entirely seriously.

I bought nothing (a personal achievement) and spent two hours browsing. The coffee shop on the main square serves cold brew over ice, which seems wrong for Greece but is correct for November and very good. Across the square, the ruins of the Roman Agora are visible through a fence. Ruins are so common in Athens that they appear casually, like punctuation, in the middle of ordinary city life.

Greek Food 101

The food in Athens is better than most people expect from a city with such a strong tourist economy. The trick is to eat where the menus are written by hand on a chalkboard and the tables are outside regardless of the month. The canonical items: taramasalata (fish roe dip), spanakopita (spinach and feta in filo, eaten for breakfast from a bakery), grilled octopus with capers and olive oil, lamb kleftiko slow-cooked until it falls off the bone. And always, always the olive oil — Athenian olive oil is viscous and green and tastes like the back of a herb garden. Pour it on everything.

The Psiri neighbourhood, slightly west of Monastiraki, has the best concentration of honest tavernas. I ate at a place with four tables and a kitchen visible through a hatch where an elderly woman cooked everything and her son served everything and the menu was three dishes depending on what had come in that day. It was the best meal of my trip.

A rooftop bar in Monastiraki at dusk — the Acropolis illuminated in the distance, the city below a grid of lights.

Rooftop Bars at Sunset

Athens has the best rooftop bar culture in Europe. The combination of a flat city with one enormous illuminated hill creates a view that is unimprovable at sunset. The rooftops in Monastiraki and Thissio are best positioned — the Acropolis appears directly ahead, golden and then lit as the sky darkens. Order retsina or an Assyrtiko white wine and stay until the lights come fully on.

A Glass (Monastiraki) and Couleur Locale (Psiri) are the two I return to. Neither requires a reservation in November. Both are full of Athenians and foreigners in roughly equal measure, all looking at the same thing with the same expression: slightly disbelieving that it exists and they are in front of it.

Practical Notes

November is ideal: mild weather, short queues, lower prices. Buy the multi-site combo ticket (€30) covering the Acropolis, Agora, Kerameikos, and other sites — it pays for itself immediately. The metro is cheap, reliable, and architecturally interesting (several stations have archaeological glass floors). Stay in Koukaki or Thissio for walkable access to the sites without the Monastiraki tourist prices.
